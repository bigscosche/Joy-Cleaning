How to Prepare Your Home
for a Cleaning Service

A little preparation goes a long way. These steps take 10–15 minutes and mean your cleaner can spend time cleaning instead of navigating clutter. Here's exactly what helps — and what you can skip entirely.

The Most Important Thing: Declutter Surfaces

This is the single most useful thing you can do before a cleaning visit. Clear countertops, coffee tables, desks, bathroom vanities, and any surfaces you want cleaned. When a surface is covered in items, the cleaner has to move everything, clean under it, and put it back — or skip it entirely to save time.

You don't need to deep-organize your home. You don't need to move furniture. Just make sure the surfaces that should be cleaned are accessible. A cleared kitchen counter takes two minutes to wipe down. A counter covered with small appliances, mail, and miscellaneous items takes much longer — and often gets a less thorough clean as a result.

Pick Up Off the Floor

Clothes on the bedroom floor, toys in the living room, shoes in the entryway — these all slow down vacuuming and mopping. The cleaner isn't going to sort through your belongings, and they shouldn't have to. A quick pass through each room to pick things up off the floor means the actual floor gets cleaned, not just cleaned around piles of stuff.

This applies especially to rugs. If a rug is covered with items, it won't get vacuumed properly. Two minutes to pick things up makes a real difference in results.

Secure Your Valuables and Fragile Items

If you have items of significant monetary or sentimental value — jewelry, heirlooms, collectibles, electronics — put them somewhere secure or clearly out of the way. This isn't a statement about trust; it's just practical. Cleaners move quickly through spaces and accidents happen. If something matters to you, don't leave it out where it could be knocked over or misplaced.

The same goes for fragile décor. If there's something delicate on a shelf that you'd rather not have touched, mention it or move it. Most cleaners will clean around items when in doubt, but the more information you give them, the better.

Handle Your Pets

If you have dogs or cats, think about where they'll be during the clean. Some pets are fine with strangers in the house; others get anxious, territorial, or underfoot in a way that makes the cleaner's job harder. A few things to consider:

  • If your dog barks at strangers or gets anxious, put them in a room or crate for the duration
  • If your cat hides on its own, that's usually fine — just let the cleaner know where it typically goes
  • If you have pets with medical needs or special instructions, mention those in advance
  • Pick up pet toys, beds, and food/water bowls so floors can be cleaned properly

Joy Cleaning uses non-toxic, pet-safe products. If you have concerns about specific products around your animals, mention it when you book.

Empty Your Dishwasher (If You Want the Sink Done)

If your sink is full of dishes or you want the area around it cleaned properly, empty the dishwasher and put dishes away before the cleaners arrive. A cleaner isn't there to do dishes — that's a separate task — but having an empty sink means the sink and surrounding counter get the attention they need.

Communicate Anything Important

Before the visit — ideally when you book — let the team know:

  • Priority areas. If your master bathroom needs extra attention or you care most about the kitchen, say so. The team can adjust accordingly.
  • Areas to skip. If there's a room or space you want left alone (a home office, a kid's room with specific organization), mention it. There's no reason to assume everything is fair game.
  • Surface materials. Natural stone countertops, hardwood floors, delicate finishes — if you have any of these, mention the material and we'll use appropriate products.
  • Specific preferences. Some people want towels folded a particular way, or prefer that items be put back where they found them rather than tidied to a different spot. These preferences are easy to accommodate once we know about them.

What You Don't Need to Do

A few things people often do before a cleaner arrives that aren't actually necessary:

  • Pre-cleaning. You're hiring someone to clean. Don't spend an hour cleaning before they arrive. Decluttering (above) is useful. Scrubbing the bathroom before the team gets there defeats the purpose.
  • Vacuuming. We bring our own vacuum and it'll be more thorough than a quick pre-visit run. Skip it.
  • Worrying about judgment. Cleaners are professionals. They've seen every kind of home in every kind of state. The messier your home is, the more clearly you'll see the impact of the work. Don't apologize for how your home looks before a clean — that's literally what we're there for.

For a First Deep Clean Specifically

If this is your first visit and you've booked a deep clean, the most important thing is access. The team needs to be able to reach appliance interiors, behind furniture, closets, and every room. Make sure all rooms are accessible and there's enough clearance around major appliances for the team to work around them.

For a deep clean, it also helps to empty the refrigerator of perishables that might spill if moved, and to make sure the oven is cooled if it's been used recently. That's it.

The Short Version

Clear surfaces. Pick up the floor. Secure anything fragile or valuable. Handle your pets. Communicate priorities. And don't pre-clean — that's our job. Everything else is secondary.

The goal is to make it easy for the team to do their best work. A few minutes of preparation on your end translates directly into a better, more thorough clean on ours.
