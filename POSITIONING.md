# Positioning

How to frame this so prospects understand what they're buying — and why it's different from everything else they've been sold.

---

## The core positioning (one sentence)

> **"I build lead generation systems for local service businesses — sites that bring in booked jobs, not just traffic. It's the website, the form, the notifications, and the dashboard, all in one."**

That's it. Everything else is how to explain it in different rooms to different audiences.

---

## Why this is NOT SEO

SEO is an input. Booked jobs are the output. Most agencies sell inputs and charge forever for them.

**SEO agency sells:**
- Keyword rankings
- Backlinks
- "Domain authority"
- Monthly retainer regardless of results

**You sell:**
- Phone calls
- Form submissions
- Booked jobs
- A tracked, auditable increase in revenue

A roofer doesn't care he ranks #3 for *"roof repair Ridgefield CT"*. He cares his phone rang 14 times last week instead of 4. You sell the phone rings. SEO is just one of the things that makes the phone ring.

---

## Why this is NOT a website

Websites are brochures. This is a lead machine that happens to live on a domain.

| Website (brochure) | Lead generation system |
|---|---|
| "Here's what we do" | "Here's how to book us right now" |
| One contact page | Every page funnels to a quote form |
| No tracking beyond Google Analytics | Every lead stored, tagged, notified |
| Updated once every 3 years | Always-on system, dashboard tracks every lead |
| Separate from operations | Directly tied to jobs and revenue |
| Designer delivers and disappears | Evolves with the business |

You're not selling a website. You're selling **what happens after the website**: the notification, the conversation, the booked job, the number that shows up in the dashboard.

---

## How to explain value in terms of jobs and revenue

Two sentences you can memorize:

> "Your average job is worth $X. If this system sends you one extra booked job per month, it pays for itself more than once. Most businesses on this system are getting 8–15 extra booked jobs a month within 90 days."

**Swap the numbers per industry:**
- Cleaner: *"Average job $175. One extra per month more than covers the monthly fee."*
- Roofer: *"Average repair $800. One extra per month covers the whole year of service."*
- Landscaper: *"Average contract $2,400. One extra contract pays for the whole system five times over."*
- HVAC: *"One install = $4,000+. Do that math."*

**Always frame cost vs. revenue. Never frame cost vs. other marketing tools.**

---

## Why this beats agencies

Agencies are built on billable hours. You're built on deliverables + outcomes.

**Agency problems:**
- Long ramp-up (6+ months to show impact)
- Bills whether or not it works
- Client doesn't own the output
- Reports are vanity (impressions, clicks, rankings)
- Hard to fire — knowledge stays with the agency
- Same template for every industry, no real customization

**Your version:**
- Site + backend + dashboard built in 2 weeks
- Client owns everything — site, database, dashboard, code
- Reports are concrete — leads in, jobs out, dollars in
- If you disappeared tomorrow, the system still works

**The line:** *"Agencies rent you marketing. I build you an asset."*

---

## Why this beats SaaS tools (HouseCallPro, Jobber, Angi, Thumbtack)

Those tools are great at what they do, but they're not the front door.

| Tool | What they do | What they don't do |
|---|---|---|
| **HouseCallPro / Jobber** | Schedule, invoice, payment | Bring leads |
| **Angi / Thumbtack / HomeAdvisor** | Sell you leads at high CPL | Let you own the customer |
| **Google Ads / Meta Ads** | Rent you attention | Build long-term equity |
| **Your system** | Owns the whole funnel: search → site → form → notification → database → dashboard | Replace scheduling tools (it feeds them) |

**The line for marketplaces:** *"Angi rents you customers. This is how you own them."*

**The line for scheduling tools:** *"Jobber runs the back. This runs the front. They plug together."*

## Simple explanation for non-technical clients

For barbecues, discovery calls, referral conversations:

> "You know how most local business websites just sit there? I build the kind that actively bring in leads. When someone searches for a roofer in your town, they land on your site, they fill out a form, and your phone buzzes right away. I also build a dashboard so you can see every lead, every job, and exactly how much you made this month."

If they push back: *"Sounds like SEO"* →

> "SEO is part of it, but SEO alone doesn't make money. I build the whole system — the site, the form, the notifications, the tracking. It's the difference between fishing and owning a fish farm."

---

## When a prospect says "I already have a website"

> "Cool. How many leads did it bring you last week?
> 
> Zero → it's a brochure.
> One → it's working, but imagine the same system doing 10× with no extra effort from you."

---

## When a prospect says "SEO is dead / AI is going to replace this"

> "Local search is actually one of the hardest things for AI to replace. When someone in your town needs a plumber right now, they're going to ask Google or their phone's assistant — and whoever's best optimized locally still wins the click. AI is just making Google smarter about matching local intent to local businesses. If anything, this system gets more valuable over time, not less."

---

## When they want to "think about it"

> "Totally fair. One thing to sit with: every month without this system is a month of leads going to whoever's already ranking in your town. The cost of waiting isn't zero — it's whatever jobs you would've booked."

---

## The posture

Don't sell SEO.
Don't sell a website.
Don't sell "marketing."
Don't sell *"traffic"*, *"rankings"*, *"engagement"*, or *"digital presence"*.

**Sell booked jobs.**

That's the business you're in. Everyone else is selling inputs and dressing them up in jargon. You're selling the output, and that's why it works.
